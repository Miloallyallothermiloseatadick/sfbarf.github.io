The Method

From August 19th to September 8th members of the SFBA Renters' Federation debated the ballot measures and voted on their endorsements online. See the online form here. Each ballot measure could be scored between 0 (hate it) and 5 (love it) or voters could pick "No Opinon/ PAC should not endorse." While voting was open, members could change our votes as often as we liked.

Discussion threads on:

  • Prop A: Housing Bond.
    Like Prop I, there was almost no discussion on the Housing Bond. It went without saying that we would support it. It raises taxes a negligible amount on property owners, to raise funds to replace housing subsidies lost when Redevelopment ended on 2012. This is a renters' organization. We have no reason to vote against tax increases on property owners.
  • Prop B: Family Leave
  • Prop C: Expenditure Lobbyists
  • Prop D: Mission Rock
  • Prop E: Requirements for Public Meetings
  • Prop F: AirBnB.
    There was no direct discussion of prop F on the list. It came up during discussions of whether we should weigh in on "non-housing" measures. Of course then we had to debate whether J and F are housing related measures or not. Despite the vocal list members arguing people should abstain or vote yes on F, F was one of our most voted on, and most hated measures.
    Based on my (Sonja here) conversations with members, opposition to F came from 4 sources:
    • Identity politics. Yes on F is part of the hostile to newcomers, hostile to tech world of SF conservatives. It's shocking and illogical to see conservatives suddenly concerned about policies that restrict supply, so long opposing the policy is also a way of being hostile to the tech industry, but not concerned about policies that restrict supply by not building in the first place. This led members to adopt a "fuck off" attitude to Yes on F. Not the best reason, but probably the most common kind of reason, on both sides of all of the ballot measures.
    • Private Right of Action. Having seen neighbors in SF destroy each other at discretionary review hearings, it's easy to believe the private right of action will be used for nefarious purposes.
    • Members use AirBnB both as hosts and guests. AirBnB adds value and convenience to users' lives.
  • Prop I: Mission Moratorium. There was no discussion on the list. The Bay Area has a housing shortage. There is no reason to not build given the shortage.
  • Prop J: Legacy Business.
    We almost achieved a "no opinion" vote, but barely voted to endorse No on J. The main reason for this also seemed to be identity politics. J is a Campos sponsored measure, and Campos also sponsored the Moratorium. Our No on J is like an exlamation point to No on I. We're so against I, that our "no"s on I overflow the I vote and show up with regard to J as well.
  • Prop K: Surplus Public Lands.
    Like the housing bond (Prop A) voting for Prop K was easy. It provides resources for affordable housing without taxing housing production.
    You may ask - why isn't Jane Kim affected by the identity politics of Campos or Yes on F? Kim sponsored the Twitter tax break. Also, her district has added a tremendous number of units. If Campos added as many units as Kim, we would like him too.

The Results

By the close of voting, if 51% of respondents voted "No Opinion ... " on a ballot proposition, the PAC did not endorse. If the average score was between 2 & 3 the PAC did not endorse. By the time of closing, 79 members voted. Final averages:

The relative sizes of the fonts in the slate card correspond to the percentage of voters that weighed in on that measure. Besides G & H, which did not receive enough votes either way to warrant an endorsement, our most tepid endorsements are of Props B & C. Only 55% of respondents weighed in on those. Our most entusiastic endorsement is No on I, followed closely by Yes on A.
